# Deep Research Assessment for the OFARM 2 Agentic AI Controlled Promotion Track

## Executive summary

OFARM’s current direction is materially aligned with the strongest part of today’s agentic-AI governance practice: manifests, traces, approvals, and simulated state should be treated as governed artefacts, not as self-authenticating truth. Current tool protocols expose schemas and authentication metadata, but they also explicitly warn that tool annotations and descriptions are untrusted unless sourced from a trusted server; they require human confirmation for sensitive operations and recommend audit logging, access controls, validation, and output sanitisation. OWASP guidance for AI agents points the same way, stressing least privilege, scoped permissions, explicit authorisation for sensitive tools, and comprehensive monitoring. citeturn20view0turn18view1turn22view0turn19view1turn19view2

The planned promotions are **not equally mature**. Tool-manifest honesty, run tracing, blocked-action tracing, and sponsor-bound actorship are close enough to promote **after targeted revision**. By contrast, advisory world-model runtime, EvidenceNeed and ObservationRequest, and farmer-facing AI workflow conformance are **not yet complete enough for full promotion**. The literature on digital twins strongly supports rigorous verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification across the twin’s full life, while NIST’s 2026 report says post-deployment AI monitoring practices and validated methodologies are still nascent. Agricultural digital-twin work also describes the field as relatively nascent and still facing major implementation challenges. citeturn34view0turn34view3turn34view4turn33view0turn33view3turn35view1

For OFARM specifically, the key governance risk is **semantic laundering**: a tool manifest treated as authority, a handoff treated as delegated approval, a world-model state treated as operational fact, a request treated as evidence or obligation, or a successful tool call treated as governance success. External standards and research do not justify any of those moves. If OFARM promotes them, it should do so only by adding explicit field-level contracts, conformance fixtures, and public-surface qualification rules that preserve the active baseline law you listed. citeturn20view0turn25view7turn19view8turn27view0turn27view3

The UX evidence also cuts against premature promotion. Farmer-facing digital advisory systems work better when advice is timely, contextualised, clear, and aligned with users’ capacities and practices. Adoption is reduced by poor connectivity, cost, limited digital literacy, low trust, and gender gaps. Separate UK evidence on agricultural decision-support shows algorithm aversion, low trust, poor usability, and unfamiliar metrics as real barriers. Alert-design literature from high-stakes decision support shows that excessive or ill-fitted alerts are ignored, and that appropriateness depends on workflow fit and the “five rights” of alerting. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0turn37view0

My overall judgement is therefore: **promote after revision**, but only as a split promotion. Promote the trace, handoff, manifest-honesty, and qualification pieces after the controlled patches below. Keep advisory world-model runtime and farmer-facing EvidenceNeed and ObservationRequest semantics in an **experiment-first** posture inside that broader recommendation until OFARM has pilot evidence for uncertainty handling, stale-data comprehension, revocation handling, and acceptable farmer burden. No recommendation below requires weakening assertion/history-first truth, adding a third AI Twin, treating agent memory as evidence, or treating world-model state as current state. citeturn33view0turn34view0turn30view0turn29view1turn29view2

## External-source findings

All external sources cited below and throughout this report were accessed on **16 May 2026**.

Current best practice for tool declaration is **descriptive, not dispositive**. MCP’s tools specification requires name, description, input schema, optional output schema, and optional annotations, but explicitly says clients must treat annotations as untrusted unless they come from trusted servers. MCP also recommends access controls, input validation, output sanitisation, user confirmation on sensitive operations, and audit logging. OpenAI’s Apps SDK adds practical machine-readable hints such as `securitySchemes`, visibility metadata, invocation status text, and `readOnlyHint`, but those are still capability descriptors rather than proof of authority or safety. In other words, today’s best practice supports honest manifests, not manifest-based trust. citeturn20view0turn18view1turn18view2turn19view1turn19view2

The main external warning is that **manifest ecosystems are already being attacked**, so OFARM should not over-read them. OWASP’s AI Agent Security Cheat Sheet describes prompt injection, tool abuse, privilege escalation, data exfiltration, excessive autonomy, approval manipulation, and cascading failures as core risks. OWASP’s Agentic Skills Top 10 goes further and treats skills and behaviour-layer manifests as a live security surface that needs isolated execution, version pinning, code signing, approval workflows, and comprehensive audit logging. Partnership on AI also notes that current model and system documentation often lacks the detail downstream adopters actually need, including security controls, performance evaluations, versioning, and limitations. citeturn22view0turn22view1turn23view1turn24view6turn24view7

For multi-agent traceability, the ecosystem provides useful components but not a sufficiency proof. A2A gives an `AgentCard` with skills and authentication requirements, task IDs, context IDs, task history, and pause states such as `input-required` and `auth-required`. OpenTelemetry defines `create_agent` and `execute_tool` spans, and its MCP semantic conventions recommend propagating `traceparent` and related context in MCP metadata. W3C PROV-O provides a clean formal basis for attributed responsibility and delegation through concepts such as `prov:Agent` and `prov:actedOnBehalfOf`. The gap is that these standards do **not** by themselves show whether approval was present, whether authority was explicitly delegated, or which rights did **not** transfer. That missing proof is exactly where OFARM’s AgentRunTrace and AgentHandoffEnvelope need to be stronger than the public protocols. citeturn25view0turn25view1turn25view2turn25view6turn25view7turn21view0turn18view3turn19view4turn18view5turn19view8

On world models and digital twins, the external evidence is very consistent: **simulation state must remain conditional, uncertainty-bearing, and lifecycle-governed**. The 2025 digital-twin VVUQ review says verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification are fundamental to safety, efficacy, and trustworthiness; previously validated versions do not guarantee that later updates remain valid; and standardised, fit-for-purpose VVUQ and full-lifecycle oversight are needed. The same literature stresses calibration, uncertainty tracking across calibration, simulation, and prediction, and the difficulty of standardising dynamic twins whose validity changes over time. NIST’s 2026 post-deployment monitoring report reinforces this, stating that best practices and validated methods for monitoring deployed AI are still nascent, yet necessary to detect drift, non-determinism, and unexpected real-world consequences. Agricultural digital-twin reviews describe the agricultural field as relatively nascent and implementation-challenged. citeturn34view0turn34view1turn34view3turn34view4turn34view5turn33view0turn33view1turn33view2turn35view1

The implications for OFARM are clear. A `WorldModelRun` is not just a “result”; it is a governed simulation event that needs model identity, calibration evidence, explicit assumptions, scenario scope, validity windows, invalidation triggers, uncertainty, and reconciliation routes back to observed outcomes. A `WorldModelState` is even more sensitive because users tend to treat state-like objects as truth-like objects. The strongest safe posture is to force these artefacts to declare themselves as Advisory Twin material, carry their horizon and uncertainty explicitly, and prohibit silent bridging into current state. That is an inference from the digital-twin and monitoring literature, but it is a strong one. citeturn34view0turn34view4turn33view3turn35view1

For farmer-facing evidence requests and AI workflows, the evidence says the system should optimise for **relevance, timing, trust, and low burden**, not maximal data collection. CGIAR’s recent human-centred agro-advisory work defines relevance through timeliness and contextualisation, and usability through clarity and alignment with farmers’ capacities and practices. The West and Central Africa digital-advisory landscape shows that connectivity cost, low literacy and digital skills, lack of support services, low trust, and gender gaps materially limit uptake. UK agricultural decision-support evidence shows that farmers can be algorithm-averse when tools are hard to understand, poorly usable, or present unfamiliar metrics; trusted human advisors remain important in adoption. High-stakes alerting research outside agriculture adds an important analogue: when alerts are excessive or poorly matched to workflow, they are overridden or ignored, and good design depends on alert appropriateness, workflow fit, and the “five rights” of who gets what alert, when, and in what form. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0turn37view0

For data sovereignty, revocation, and machine-readable sharing, external practice is also relatively coherent. NIST ABAC makes authorisation a function of subject, object, operation, and environment attributes, which is a strong fit for OFARM’s explicit authority and freshness gates. W3C ODRL provides a machine-readable way to express permissions, prohibitions, and duties for assets. OAuth revocation invalidates tokens and can invalidate related tokens under the same grant. NIST’s Privacy Framework calls for revocable authorisations, data-processing permissions travelling with data, audit logs, and accessible records of disclosure and sharing. GDPR requires demonstrable consent, easy withdrawal, erasure rights, data-protection-by-design, and records of processing. The EU Data Act says the user of a connected product should enjoy access to generated data, without overriding personal-data law. These sources do not tell OFARM exactly what schema to use, but they clearly justify explicit policy bindings, revocation propagation, re-checking before disclosure, and durable audit records. citeturn19view9turn18view9turn18view7turn30view0turn29view0turn29view1turn29view2turn29view3turn18view8turn28view0

Finally, the strongest external comparator for runtime readiness and auditability remains “document more, monitor after deployment, and do not overclaim.” NIST’s generative-AI profile recommends provenance, version history, human oversight roles, risk inventories, and post-deployment monitoring with appeal, override, incident response, and change management. NIST’s draft documentation work and PAI’s enterprise work both emphasise that useful downstream documentation must include machine-readable and human-readable artefacts, versioning, intended use, known limitations, evaluation metrics, uncertainty, monitoring, and update mechanisms. The EU AI Act, while not a direct one-to-one template for OFARM, is directionally relevant in requiring automatic logging over system lifetime and human oversight proportionate to risk, autonomy, and context of use. citeturn31view0turn31view2turn31view3turn31view4turn31view5turn24view0turn24view2turn24view4turn24view5turn27view0turn27view3

## Do-not-promote warnings and hostile tests

**Warnings**

Do not promote any manifest, capability catalogue, `AgentCard`, or skill descriptor into an implicit trust or authority object. External protocols and guidance treat these artefacts as descriptors that still need validation, approval, and monitoring. In OFARM terms, a manifest may describe a tool; it must not grant authority, settle safety, or count as governance success. citeturn20view0turn18view1turn22view0turn22view1

Do not promote any handoff model in which authority silently “comes along for the ride.” A2A can carry task history, status, identity requirements, and context, but it does not itself prove bounded delegation. Silent authority transfer would directly violate OFARM’s sponsor-bound authority principle and should remain prohibited. citeturn25view1turn25view2turn25view7turn19view8

Do not promote advisory world-model runtime as if VVUQ were already solved for agricultural decision support. The best external literature says the opposite: validation and uncertainty governance are central, updates can invalidate prior validation, and post-deployment monitoring for AI remains methodologically immature. Promote the contracts first; keep stronger readiness claims out of baseline law for now. citeturn34view0turn34view4turn33view0turn33view2turn35view1

Do not promote EvidenceNeed or ObservationRequest into implicit obligations, evidence, or compliance blockers. The UX and alerting evidence says that low-value or poorly contextualised prompts reduce trust and uptake; the legal and privacy evidence says authorisations, disclosures, and consent should be explicit and revocable. OFARM should therefore make “request” semantics intentionally weak unless another explicit rule gives them force. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0turn37view0turn30view0turn29view1

Do not promote “runtime readiness”, “two-agent compatibility”, “world-model readiness”, or “autonomous compliance support” as public claims without production evidence. NIST’s current position is that post-deployment monitoring best practices and validated methodologies are still nascent, which means these claims are easy to overstate. citeturn33view0turn31view4turn24view5

**Hostile tests to add**

- Manifest says `read-only`, but the tool writes to a remote system anyway.
- Manifest omits network egress to a third-party domain used during execution.
- Tool call returns success, but the required approval was absent or expired.
- Tool call succeeds, but policy re-check at answer time would have blocked disclosure.
- Handoff carries sponsor identity but omits which rights transferred and which did not.
- Recipient agent acts on sender authority without explicit acceptance and revalidation.
- Blocked action is hidden from the trace because the action never executed.
- Agent memory snippet is cited as if it were evidence pack content.
- Cache or projection is used to answer a question after revocation changed permissions.
- World-model state is materialised into current state without a bridged and accepted governance event.
- Advisory scenario output is shown later in a daily brief as if it were current operational truth.
- ObservationRequest auto-closes from a malformed upload or empty photo.
- EvidenceNeed escalates to a blocker even though no external rule or gate justifies blocking.
- Offline contractor sync replays a once-authorised share after revocation.
- Public brief strips redacted fields from detail view but leaks them in summary text.
- ResultQualificationEnvelope reports “qualified” because the tool succeeded, even though freshness failed.
- Trace says two agents are compatible because protocol versions match, while approval, scope, or sandbox assumptions differ.
- Dispute reconstruction fails because assumptions, freshness snapshot, or governing pack refs are missing.

These hostile tests are directly motivated by the tool-safety, approval, revocation, delegation, and monitoring risks documented across MCP, OWASP, NIST, A2A, and privacy-governance guidance. citeturn20view0turn22view0turn21view0turn25view7turn33view0turn30view0

## Minimum machine-readable fields

The field sets below are intentionally **minimum**, not maximal. They are designed to keep the artefacts honest, testable, and non-magical in the specific OFARM sense: no hidden truth, no hidden authority, no hidden governance. The field choices are derived from the combined pressures of schema-based tool description, trace propagation, provenance, VVUQ, revocation, and post-deployment monitoring. citeturn20view0turn21view0turn19view8turn24view0turn24view2turn24view5turn30view0

**`WorldModelRun`** — `world_model_run_id`, `twin_ref`, `scenario_id`, `scenario_type`, `model_id`, `model_version`, `model_card_ref`, `calibration_pack_ref`, `input_observation_refs`, `input_assumption_set_ref`, `parameter_overrides`, `run_timestamp`, `simulation_horizon`, `validity_window`, `uncertainty_summary`, `invalidation_triggers`, `reconciliation_plan_ref`, `advisory_only`, `current_state_bridge_ref`, `public_surface_policy_ref`. ## Farmer-facing UX requirements

Every farmer-facing surface should display qualification before content, not after content. Each brief, recommendation, request, preflight result, or shared artefact should carry compact, always-visible status markers for: **Advisory only or compliance-related**, **fresh at / stale since**, **missing evidence**, **permission-limited**, **approval needed**, and **last human-verified step if any**. This is the smallest interface move that directly addresses the trust and clarity problems identified in the agricultural and alerting literature. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0turn37view0

Daily briefs should be the default home for low-urgency EvidenceNeed and ObservationRequest traffic. Interruptive prompts should be reserved for true timing sensitivity, safety risk, or explicit compliance gates that already exist outside the request itself. Requests should be batched by field, task, or day; duplicates should collapse; and each item should tell the farmer why it matters, what will change if it is ignored, how much effort it will take, and by when it is most useful. That is the practical response to alert-fatigue and farmer-burden evidence. citeturn37view0turn18view11turn36view2

Evidence capture should support alternative routes because rural workflows are messy. If a geo-tagged photo is best, the interface should say so; if a short note, voice clip, contractor upload, or later sync is still sufficient, it should allow that too. The interface should also show a burden estimate, a relevance window, and whether the request is advisory-helpful, preflight-relevant, or genuinely gate-relevant under another rule. A request that cannot say that in plain language should not be tasking a farmer. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0

Compliance preflight and output restriction UX should surface limits without overwhelming the user. The recommended pattern is a one-line qualification summary with an expand-for-detail view: what evidence was used, what is missing, what assumptions remained, which permissions constrained the answer, which approvals are still required, and whether the result may be shared. This is consistent with the general uncertainty literature that uncertainty should indicate when an output should not be trusted or should be escalated to a human. citeturn37view3turn31view4turn27view3

Contractor and offline-sync workflows need a visibly different state model from live verified workflows. Unsynced captures should be labelled **captured locally**, not **accepted**. Locally available recommendations that depend on stale or unsynchronised state should be labelled accordingly. Any share action should require a fresh policy check after sync, not before. This is the practical consequence of the revocation, logging, and Data Act or GDPR style of rights-aware processing. citeturn18view7turn30view0turn29view1turn28view0

Dispute reconstruction should be available as a time-ordered timeline that a non-technical farmer can actually read. At minimum it should show: what the system knew, what it assumed, what it asked for, what it simulated, what it was allowed to see, what it was not allowed to share, and which approval or governance gate changed the outcome. Without that, farmer-facing explainability becomes theatre. The external traceability and provenance sources strongly support this timeline-style reconstruction. citeturn21view0turn19view8turn31view7

## Remaining uncertainty

The standards landscape OFARM would be borrowing from is still moving. MCP is relatively concrete on tool structure and safety, but A2A is still early-versioned, and OpenTelemetry’s GenAI and MCP conventions are explicitly marked as developmental in important areas. OFARM should therefore avoid binding its baseline law too tightly to any single external protocol’s field names. It should instead import the governance pattern and keep its own machine contract stable. citeturn20view0turn18view4turn18view3turn21view0

The strongest empirical gap is in **post-deployment monitoring for agentic agricultural workflows**. NIST’s 2026 monitoring report is explicit that best practices, validated methodologies, and common terminology are still nascent. That means OFARM can define conformance expectations and evidence thresholds now, but threshold tuning for drift, stale-state burden, revocation latency, and handoff safety will still need pilot data. citeturn33view0turn33view2turn33view4

The farmer UX evidence base is decent for digital advisory adoption, trust, clarity, connectivity, literacy, and algorithm aversion, but still thin for complex multi-agent agricultural workflows with explicit authority and governance gates. Some of the recommended request-burden and alerting controls therefore rely on careful inference from mature decision-support literature outside agriculture. That inference is reasonable, but it should still be treated as something to validate in pilots rather than as settled science. citeturn18view11turn36view2turn36view0turn37view0

Farm-data sovereignty is also not fully settled in a simple one-rule way. The EU agricultural data-sharing code is explicitly non-binding, while the Data Act creates stronger access rights around connected-product data but still leaves personal-data law intact. OFARM can safely require explicit sharing, policy expression, revocation, and audit; it cannot assume that one simple “farm owns all data” label will be legally sufficient across contexts. citeturn18view10turn28view0turn18view8

## Recommendation

**Promote after revision.**

More precisely, promote the package **in two bands**. The run-trace, blocked-action, sponsor-bound actorship, handoff, manifest-honesty, result-qualification, and hostile-runtime-conformance pieces are mature enough to promote after the narrow revisions above. The advisory world-model runtime, EvidenceNeed and ObservationRequest semantics, and farmer-facing AI workflow conformance should remain in an **experiment-first gate within that broader recommendation** until OFARM has limited live evidence for uncertainty display, invalidation behaviour, revocation handling, stale-data comprehension, request burden, and dispute reconstruction. citeturn33view0turn34view0turn35view1turn18view11turn36view0turn37view0

Claims that should remain prohibited for now are: **runtime ready**, **two-agent compatible**, **world-model ready**, and **autonomous compliance support**, unless backed by production implementation evidence, live deployment telemetry, post-deployment monitoring, and conformance fixtures that specifically prove no hidden authority, no hidden truth, no silent delegation, and no silent leakage after revocation. That is the conservative conclusion most consistent with current best practice. citeturn31view4turn24view5turn27view0turn27view3turn30view0
